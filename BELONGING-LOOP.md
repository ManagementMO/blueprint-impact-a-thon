# The Belonging Loop

## From "I want to go" to "I was part of it"

The Belonging Loop is an accessible, person-directed community participation network for Waterloo Region. It does not stop at listing events. It connects the full journey:

**Wish -> Match -> Understand -> Arrange support -> Join the event circle -> Contribute -> Attend -> Remember -> Improve what happens next**

It combines seven ideas into one product:

1. **Wish First** - people express what they want before agencies decide what to offer.
2. **Before I Go** - every event explains its first few minutes and its access facts.
3. **AI Coordination Exchange** - coordinators optimize paid worker shifts and match verified helpers to bounded support needs.
4. **Event Circles** - a Luma-adjacent event page with opt-in attendance visibility, host announcements, questions, and moderated chat.
5. **Bring Together Board** - attendees claim optional contributions such as cake, icing, games, supplies, or setup help.
6. **Event Memories** - consent-aware photos, comments, questions, and reflections live with the event rather than in an infinite global feed.
7. **Collective Momentum** - cooperative, optional rewards make preparation and participation satisfying without manipulative streaks or public leaderboards.

The core idea is simple:

> Event discovery is not participation. Participation happens when a person finds something they want, understands what will happen, has the right support, knows who will be there, has a meaningful way to take part, and leaves with a stronger connection.

---

## The One-Sentence Pitch

**The Belonging Loop helps a person say what they want, find or shape the right event, know exactly what to expect, arrange trusted support, connect with the event community, contribute in their own way, and carry that connection into the next experience.**

## Why This Fits KW Habilitation

The competition asks teams to demonstrate both sides of the system:

- How a community member discovers an event.
- How a nonprofit posts one.

It also weights problem fit, demonstrated accessibility, feasibility, and originality equally. The organizers explicitly warn against stopping at another filterable calendar.

KW Habilitation's broader direction makes the real objective clearer:

- Support should be person-directed.
- People should build confidence, independence, relationships, and natural community connections.
- The Belonging Collective should create collaboration rather than competition between agencies.
- The goal is not simply participation. It is connection.

The current Teamup calendar is useful inventory, but inventory alone does not answer:

- Is this something I actually want?
- What will happen when I arrive?
- Can my needs be supported?
- Who can help me attend safely?
- Will I know anyone?
- Can I contribute instead of only consuming a service?
- What happens to the relationship after the event?

The Belonging Loop is the layer that answers those questions while leaving existing registration systems in place.

---

## The Core Product Object: An Event Circle

Every event becomes an **Event Circle**, not just a listing.

An Event Circle contains:

- The event facts and external registration link.
- A Before I Go arrival and access preview.
- The host organization and partner organizations.
- Opt-in attendance visibility.
- Host announcements and moderated questions.
- Paid support roles and verified helper needs.
- Optional things attendees can bring or do.
- A consent-aware event memory feed.
- A clear owner and last-confirmed date for every access fact.

This single object prevents the product from becoming seven unrelated features. Wishes lead into Event Circles, staff and helpers coordinate around Event Circles, and social interaction remains scoped to Event Circles.

# The Belonging Loop, Step by Step

## 1. Wish First: Let the Community Direct the Calendar

Most event platforms are supply-directed: agencies publish what they already decided to offer, then people search through it.

Wish First reverses that relationship.

A person answers three short questions using text, pictures, audio, switch access, or support from someone they trust:

1. **What do you want to do?**
2. **When and where could it work?**
3. **What would help you take part?**

Example:

> I want to go on an accessible nature walk on Saturday. I prefer a small group. I need trained mobility support on uneven ground.

The platform searches existing events first.

- If a strong match exists, the person receives one clear invitation with an explanation.
- If no strong match exists, the result is not a dead-end page. With consent, the wish becomes an anonymous demand signal.
- Similar wishes are grouped only after a privacy threshold is reached.
- Partner agencies can respond individually or co-create an event.

The person is never promised that every wish will be fulfilled. The system shows honest states such as:

- Match available.
- Partner reviewing.
- More people expressed a similar wish.
- No response yet.
- Wish expired; renew if still relevant.

### Why nonprofits contribute

- They can see demand before spending program funds.
- They can plan around real preferred times, places, and access needs.
- They can find partners with complementary space, staff, or expertise.
- They gain evidence for funding applications and program planning.
- They are more likely to fill programs designed around expressed interest.

---

## 2. Before I Go: Replace "Accessible" With Useful Facts

An event should never receive a universal "accessible" badge. Accessibility depends on the person, the environment, the support available, and the accuracy of the information.

Before I Go gives a person a factual preview of the first few minutes:

- Which entrance to use.
- What the surface and route are like.
- How check-in works.
- What happens first.
- The host role or fallback contact.
- Expected noise, crowd, light, pace, and predictability.
- Whether a quiet or lower-stimulation space exists.
- Whether an accessible washroom is available.
- Support-person rules and costs.
- Whether leaving early is okay.
- What remains unknown.

Every fact carries provenance:

- **Confirmed** - verified by the hosting organization.
- **Reported** - supplied by a partner or prior attendee.
- **Not known** - no one has confirmed it.
- **Last checked** - date and responsible organization.

This is an access-facts layer, not a certification or guarantee.

### Why nonprofits contribute

- Fewer repetitive access questions.
- Fewer mismatches and preventable no-shows.
- A reusable venue profile for recurring events.
- Clear ownership of facts instead of vague claims.
- Better AODA-aligned communication without pretending one format serves everyone.

---

## 3. AI Coordination Exchange

The AI pairing idea must be split into two systems because paid care and community help have different legal, safety, labour, and trust requirements.

## 3A. Paid Worker Shift Optimization

This is a coordinator-facing tool for participating caregiving organizations.

It recommends shift coverage using constraints such as:

- Worker availability.
- Employer and approved partner organization.
- Role, certification, and training.
- Person-specific continuity preferences.
- Communication skills and language.
- Location and travel time.
- Maximum hours, rest requirements, and labour rules.
- Support ratios.
- Budget and funding source.
- Supervision requirements.
- Conflicts of interest.

The system returns:

- A ranked set of feasible assignments.
- The reason each match is feasible.
- Any violated or unresolved constraint.
- Travel and overtime implications.
- A fallback plan.

**AI recommends; an authorized coordinator approves.** The system never autonomously assigns care, changes a shift, or shares a worker's schedule with another organization.

### Partnership model

Participating caregiving organizations can form a controlled capacity network:

- Each organization exposes only approved capacity windows, not full staff rosters.
- Cross-organization requests require a memorandum of understanding.
- Credentials, rates, insurance, supervision, cancellation, and incident ownership are agreed in advance.
- A worker remains employed and supervised through an authorized organization.
- The person receiving support can accept or decline the proposed arrangement.

## 3B. Verified Helper Matching

This is not an open gig marketplace.

Potential helpers come from a vetted, organization-managed pool with:

- Identity verification.
- Screening appropriate to the role.
- Role-specific training.
- Clear physical-task boundaries.
- Availability and location.
- Insurance and liability coverage where required.
- An accountable supervising organization.
- Reporting and removal processes.

For the hiking example, the platform would not match a random attendee to "push a wheelchair." It would define a specific **mobility-support role for outdoor terrain**, identify required training and physical-task limits, and recommend only approved people. The event coordinator verifies the route and assignment, and the participant explicitly accepts the support plan.

If no safe match exists, the event is shown as **support not arranged**, not as ready to attend.

### Matching principles

- Match the requested task, not a diagnosis.
- Collect only the minimum information needed.
- Explain why a person was suggested.
- Let the coordinator and participant reject the suggestion.
- Never rank helpers by popularity or social status.
- Never treat volunteer help as a substitute for required paid care.

---

## 4. Event Circles: Luma-Adjacent, but Designed for Trust

Each Event Circle has a social layer before, during, and after the event.

### Before the event

- Opt-in "Going" or "Interested" status.
- Anonymous attendance count by default.
- Optional visibility to approved circle members.
- Host announcements.
- A moderated event chat.
- A question board where answers can become reusable event facts.
- Polls such as music choice, food preference, or activity theme.
- A visible host role and moderation status.

### During the event

- Arrival update from the host.
- Contribution checklist.
- Changes to room, entrance, schedule, or support.
- A clear help/report action.

### After the event

- Photos with explicit media consent.
- Comments and questions.
- A short private reflection: comfortable, connected, want to return, need something different.
- An invitation to a related event only when the person opts in.

### What it deliberately does not copy from mainstream social media

- No infinite global feed.
- No public follower counts.
- No public popularity ranking.
- No default direct messages.
- No location sharing outside the event's needs.
- No pressure to expose attendance.
- No engagement optimization based on vulnerability.

The event is the social context. The product helps relationships form around shared participation rather than asking people to build a public persona.

---

## 5. Bring Together Board

Every Event Circle can include optional items or roles.

Examples:

- I will bring the cake.
- I will bring the icing.
- I can bring a board game.
- I can choose three songs.
- I can welcome newcomers with the host.
- I can help set out nonessential supplies.
- I can bring extra sunscreen or bottled water.
- I can take photos only of people who opted in.

Each contribution has:

- What is needed.
- Quantity.
- Who owns the request.
- Claimed, unclaimed, or completed state.
- Accessibility or transport notes.
- Reminder timing.
- A private decline or release option.

### Hard boundary

Essential safety, accessibility, medication, transportation, food-allergy, and care responsibilities remain owned by the host or authorized support organization. They are never crowdsourced through the contribution board.

Participation is never conditional on contributing. "Just attend" is always an equal option.

---

## 6. Event Memories

Post-event content creates continuity without turning the product into an unrestricted social network.

An Event Circle can retain:

- Approved photos.
- Captions and alt text.
- Participant comments.
- Questions for the host.
- A short event recap.
- Things the group wants to do next.
- Access facts that should be corrected.

### Media consent model

- Consent is specific to the event and media type.
- "I can be photographed" is separate from "my photo can be posted."
- A person can participate without appearing in media.
- Hosts can mark photo-safe zones or times.
- Uploaders confirm that visible people consented.
- Moderators can remove content immediately.
- The retention period is explicit.
- Revoked consent triggers removal from future display.
- Face recognition is not used.

The product should offer blur or crop assistance, but AI must not infer whether someone consented.

---

## 7. Collective Momentum: Rewarding, Not Addictive

An "addictive XP system" would be the wrong goal for a disability-services product. The healthier objective is a loop people enjoy returning to because it creates agency, recognition, and connection.

The recommended model is **cooperative momentum**:

- An Event Readiness meter fills as access facts are confirmed, questions are answered, and optional contributions are claimed.
- A shared Community Goal recognizes collective actions across partner organizations.
- Personal recognition is private or opt-in.
- Rewards are small, social, and nonmonetary: choose next month's theme, add a song to the playlist, help name a new event, or unlock a collective celebration.
- Progress never expires because someone missed a day.

### Actions that may receive recognition

- Adding an accurate access fact.
- Answering an unanswered event question.
- Claiming and completing an optional contribution.
- Sharing useful post-event feedback.
- Helping choose a future event.
- Welcoming someone through an approved host role.

### Actions that must not be gamified

- Receiving care.
- Disclosing a disability or support need.
- Taking medication.
- Attending despite discomfort.
- Performing physical assistance.
- Uploading photos of other people.
- Maintaining a streak.

No public individual leaderboard, loot-box mechanic, artificial scarcity, or streak penalty should exist.

# Full User Journey

## Maya's Journey

Maya wants to join an accessible outdoor event but finds unfamiliar groups stressful and needs mobility support on uneven terrain.

1. Maya uses picture and spoken choices to say: nature, Saturday, small group, mobility support.
2. The platform looks for existing events before creating a wish.
3. It finds an upcoming partner-hosted trail event.
4. Maya opens Before I Go and sees the entrance, trail surface, check-in steps, quiet break point, accessible washroom status, and which facts remain unknown.
5. The event currently says "mobility support not arranged."
6. The host requests a defined outdoor mobility-support role.
7. The AI suggests one available paid worker and two verified helpers who satisfy the approved requirements.
8. A coordinator reviews the recommendation and proposes one arrangement.
9. Maya or her authorized supporter accepts it.
10. Maya joins the Event Circle without making her attendance public.
11. She reads the host announcements, asks whether the group stops for breaks, and sees the answer added to the event facts.
12. She claims the optional role "choose the first trail photo prompt."
13. After the event, Maya decides whether one approved photo can be posted and privately says she would attend again.

This one journey connects discovery, accessibility, staffing, social confidence, contribution, and continuity.

# Nonprofit and Partner Journey

1. A host imports or creates an event.
2. The host answers four required Before I Go questions.
3. The host identifies any unknown access facts instead of guessing.
4. The host sets attendance visibility and chat moderation rules.
5. The host lists paid support requirements separately from optional helper roles.
6. The host can invite a partner organization to co-host or supply approved capacity.
7. An authorized coordinator reviews AI pairing suggestions.
8. The host adds optional contribution items.
9. The event publishes to the existing calendar and its Event Circle.
10. Questions, attendance intent, staffing status, and contribution claims update the coordinator view.
11. Afterward, the host approves media, responds to feedback, and corrects reusable venue facts.

# Data and Fields

## Event

- Title and plain-language title.
- Host organization.
- Partner organizations.
- Date, time, recurrence, and timezone.
- Structured location and virtual option.
- Cost and support-person cost.
- Drop-in or registration required.
- External registration link and deadline.
- Intended audience stated factually.
- Capacity and current availability.
- Event owner and last-updated date.

## Before I Go Access Facts

- Correct entrance.
- Step-free route.
- Terrain or surface.
- Accessible parking or drop-off.
- Accessible washroom status.
- Noise, crowd, lighting, and pace.
- Quiet or lower-stimulation space.
- Communication supports and languages.
- Support-person policy.
- Service-animal information.
- Check-in steps.
- What happens first.
- Whether leaving early is okay.
- Host role or fallback contact.
- Source, confidence state, owner, and last-confirmed date.

- Interest or desired activity.
- Preferred date or time window.
- Neighbourhood or travel limit.
- Cost limit.
- Group-size preference.
- Environment preference.
- Support task needed.
- Communication mode.
- Expiry date.
- Consent to aggregate anonymously.

- Specific task.
- Paid worker required or verified helper eligible.
- Training or certification required.
- Physical-task boundary.
- Duration and location.
- Ratio and supervision.

# What the AI Actually Does

The product should not use "AI" as a vague label. Different jobs need different methods.

## Appropriate AI or optimization tasks

- Rank existing events against explicitly chosen preferences.
- Group anonymous wishes into demand patterns.
- Convert event facts into plain-language, audio, and visual formats for human approval.
- Suggest paid worker assignments using a constraint solver.
- Suggest vetted helpers using hard eligibility filters before ranking.
- Detect unresolved scheduling conflicts.
- Summarize event questions for the host.
- Flag potentially unsafe or abusive content for human moderation.
- Suggest alt text that the uploader confirms.

## Tasks AI must never do autonomously

- Diagnose or infer a disability.
- Decide what a person is capable of.
- Assign paid care.
- Approve a volunteer for physical support.
- Override labour, insurance, training, or supervision rules.
- Promise that an access need will be met.
- Expose worker schedules or participant needs to unauthorized partners.
- Decide media consent from a photo.
- Remove or punish a user without human review except for immediate technical containment.

Every recommendation should explain its decisive factors and unresolved uncertainty.

# Trust, Safety, and Governance

## Paid care and helpers

- Separate paid support from optional community help.
- Require accountable organizations, vetting, training, and role boundaries.
- Use public meeting points and approved event locations.
- Keep incident reporting and emergency response outside chat.
- Give participants and workers a no-penalty rejection option.

## Privacy

- No diagnosis is needed for event matching.
- Needs are disclosed only to roles that require them.
- Wishes are aggregated only above a privacy threshold.
- Attendance is private by default.
- Cross-organization staffing exposes capacity, not a full roster.
- Direct messaging is off by default.

## Moderation

- Each Event Circle has an accountable host and moderator.
- Report, mute, block, and remove controls are accessible.
- Questions can be asked privately when appropriate.
- Moderation decisions are logged and appealable.
- The platform is not an emergency or crisis service.

## Accuracy

- Access facts have an owner and last-confirmed date.
- Unknown is a valid state.
- Recurring events prompt periodic reconfirmation.
- A changed venue invalidates location-specific facts.

## Co-design

The product must be validated with self-advocates, direct support professionals, families, workers, accessibility specialists, and partner organizations. A prototype can demonstrate the interaction and technical approach; it cannot certify that the full system is accessible or operationally safe.

# The Hackathon-Winning Prototype

The full vision is too large for one day. The prototype should tell one complete story and make the larger system believable.

## Build live

1. **Wish First flow** - three accessible questions leading to one event.
2. **Before I Go card** - entrance, first steps, environment, support status, and honest unknowns.
3. **Nonprofit posting flow** - four structured access questions and one support need.
4. **AI pairing explanation** - one paid-worker suggestion and one verified-helper suggestion, clearly marked as coordinator-approved mock data.
5. **Event Circle preview** - opt-in attendance count, one moderated question, and one contribution claim.

## Show as a static future-state screen

- Post-event photo and comment flow.
- Cooperative Event Readiness meter.
- Cross-organization capacity view.

## Do not build during the hackathon

- A real workforce scheduling engine.
- A real volunteer marketplace.
- Background checks or credential verification.
- Registration or payment.
- Full chat infrastructure.
- A global social feed.
- Real cross-organization data exchange.
- An XP economy.

Those features belong in the product vision and handoff, not in the critical demo path.

# 60-Second Closed-Laptop Pitch

> A calendar can tell Maya that a hike exists. It cannot tell her whether she will feel ready, have the right support, know anyone, or be part of the experience. The Belonging Loop starts with Maya, not the schedule. She says by pictures or voice: "Nature, Saturday, small group, mobility support." We find one event and show its first few minutes: the entrance, terrain, check-in, quiet-break option, and what is still unknown. The host posts those facts and requests a trained support role. AI suggests only approved workers or verified helpers; a coordinator and Maya decide. Then her event circle shows host updates, optional attendees, questions, and ways to contribute. # Judging Rubric Fit

## Problem fit

- Covers both required flows: member discovery and nonprofit posting.
- Preserves existing external registration.
- Gives agencies a concrete reason to contribute.
- Turns inter-agency collaboration into staffing, co-hosting, and shared event support.

## Demonstrated accessibility

- Starts with a specific person's chosen needs, not a generic accessible mode.
- Supports picture, text, audio, keyboard, switch, and screen-reader paths.
- Replaces vague claims with structured facts and honest unknowns.
- Requires real-user validation rather than claiming universal access.

## Thinking and feasibility

- Uses the existing calendar as an event source.
- Keeps the live prototype to one vertical slice.
- Separates paid support, verified helpers, and ordinary attendees.
- Makes AI explainable and human-approved.
- Clearly distinguishes prototype, pilot, and production requirements.

## Originality

- Reverses discovery by letting demand shape programming.
- Extends the product beyond search into arrival, staffing, contribution, and continuity.
- Makes collaboration operational rather than decorative.
- Uses an event-scoped social network instead of another global feed.

# Success Metrics

The north star should not be clicks or time in the app.

## North-star outcome

**People who attend and voluntarily report one new or strengthened community connection.**

## Supporting metrics

- Wish-to-existing-match rate.
- Unmet wishes that reach an anonymous planning threshold.
- Cross-organization events created in response to demand.
- Access-fact completeness and age.
- Event questions answered before start time.
- Registration-to-attendance rate where partners can share aggregate data.
- First-timer return intention.
- Support requests safely filled.
- Coordinator time to fill a shift.
- Optional contributions claimed and completed.
- Content reports and resolution time.
- Media-consent violations.
- Participant-reported comfort, agency, and connection.

No metric should reward disclosure, compulsory attendance, or excessive app use.

# Major Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Unfulfilled wishes create false hope | Match existing events first; use honest statuses, thresholds, expiry, and no promise of creation. |
| Wrong access facts cause harm | Named owner, provenance, last-confirmed date, unknown state, and fallback contact. |
| AI makes an unsafe support match | Hard eligibility filters, coordinator approval, participant acceptance, and accountable organization. |
| Volunteer labour replaces paid care | Explicit role classification and prohibition on using volunteers for required regulated care. |
| Chat exposes vulnerable people | Event-scoped visibility, no default DMs, accountable moderators, report/block tools, and minimal profiles. |
| Photos violate consent | Event-specific media consent, no-consent participation path, moderator approval, removal workflow, and no face recognition. |
| Gamification becomes coercive | Cooperative, optional recognition with no streaks, leaderboards, scarcity, or rewards tied to care. |
| Staff cannot maintain another system | Import existing events, reuse venue facts, keep required fields small, and assign ownership. |
| Cross-organization partnership stalls | Pilot within one organization first, then add capacity-sharing through explicit agreements. |
| The concept is too broad for the competition | Demo one complete journey; place scheduling, social feed, and XP depth in the roadmap. |

# Rollout Path

## Phase 0 - Co-design and governance

- Validate the wish vocabulary and access facts with self-advocates.
- Define paid-care and helper role boundaries.
- Establish media consent and moderation policy.
- Agree on a shared event data dictionary.

## Phase 1 - Event Circles and Before I Go

- Import existing calendar events.
- Add access facts, provenance, questions, and event-scoped announcements.
- Pilot with one venue and one recurring event.

## Phase 2 - Wish First

- Match existing events.
- Add privacy-preserving unmet-demand aggregation.
- Give agencies a planning dashboard.

## Phase 3 - Internal shift optimization

- Start inside one caregiving organization.
- Use hard constraints, explainability, and coordinator approval.
- Measure scheduling time, continuity, overtime, and safety.

## Phase 4 - Verified helper and partner capacity network

- Add approved helper pools.
- Sign cross-organization operating agreements.
- Pilot one bounded, low-risk support role before physical assistance.

## Phase 5 - Contributions, memories, and collective momentum

- Add optional bring/do roles.
- Add consent-aware event memories.
- Test cooperative recognition with self-advocates before wider release.

# Nonprofit Q&A

## "Who is responsible if the AI gets a support match wrong?"

The accountable organization remains responsible. AI only suggests options after hard eligibility checks. An authorized coordinator approves, and the participant can decline.

## "Can anyone volunteer to provide physical assistance?"

No. Physical support is a defined role with organization-approved vetting, training, task boundaries, insurance, supervision, and participant consent.

## "Are you replacing our registration systems?"

No. Registration stays with each organization. The Belonging Loop handles discovery, access facts, support readiness, and the event community.

## "How do you protect attendance and disability information?"

Attendance is private by default, the system stores needs rather than diagnoses, disclosure is role-based, and cross-organization staffing shares approved capacity rather than full rosters.

## "What if an event is not accessible?"

The product does not label events universally accessible. It shows factual, sourced information and unknowns so the person can decide whether the event fits.

## "Why would another organization contribute?"

They gain validated demand, fuller programs, fewer repeated questions, better planning evidence, access to controlled partner capacity, and measurable cross-agency collaboration.

## "Who moderates chat and photos?"

Every Event Circle has an accountable host organization and named moderator. Posting and media rules are explicit, and people can participate without chat or photos.

## "Isn't this too much for one day?"

The vision is large; the prototype is intentionally small: one wish, one event, one arrival preview, one nonprofit post, one explained support suggestion, and one event-circle interaction.

# Final Positioning

The Belonging Loop is not:

- Another filterable calendar.
- A generic AI chatbot.
- An unvetted volunteer marketplace.
- An autonomous care scheduler.
- A global social network.
- A disability leaderboard.

It is:

- A person-directed demand signal.
- A factual access and arrival layer.
- A controlled coordination tool for organizations.
- A trusted event-scoped community.
- A way for people to contribute on their own terms.
- A feedback loop that helps the Collective plan what should exist next.

**The calendar answers "What is happening?" The Belonging Loop answers "Can I belong there, what will make it possible, and what connection comes next?"**
