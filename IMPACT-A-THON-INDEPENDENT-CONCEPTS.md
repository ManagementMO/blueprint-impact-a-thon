## Executive Recommendation

There are three especially strong directions:

1. **Listen First** is the highest-upside, most contrarian concept. It reverses
   the calendar so community demand can shape programming.
2. **First Five** has the highest probability of placing. It is extremely
   feasible, demonstrates accessibility directly, and has a clean demo.
3. **Needed Here** is the strongest philosophical wildcard. It reframes
   belonging as meaningful contribution rather than passive attendance.

Do not build all three. Pick one core concept and execute one polished vertical
slice. A small Access Facts component can support either Listen First or First
Five without becoming a separate product.

## 1. What the Competition Actually Rewards

### Day and pitch format

- 10:30 AM: opening ceremonies
- 11:00 AM: working time begins
- 1:00 PM: lunch
- 5:00 PM: submissions and pitches begin
- 6:30 PM: closing ceremonies

Round 1 is a 60-second pitch with laptops closed. An open laptop during the
pitch is grounds for disqualification.

The top six teams receive a 2.5-minute demo and one minute of nonprofit
questions. The top three place.

### Required output

The prototype must show both:

1. How a community member discovers an event.
2. How a nonprofit posts an event.

Registration can remain in each nonprofit's existing external system. Building
a unified registration platform is unnecessary and outside the intended scope.

The handoff document must explain:

- What was built and what comes next
- Important decisions and their reasoning
- What remains unsolved
- Stack, links, ownership, and continuation steps
- Accessibility addressed in the prototype
- Accessibility that still requires testing with real users

### Judging criteria

Each category is worth 25 percent:

- Problem fit
- User and demonstrated accessibility
- Thinking and feasibility
- Originality in discovery and collaboration

Originality cannot rescue a concept that is inaccessible or impossible for KW
Hab to continue. Conversely, a polished but conventional calendar is unlikely
to stand out.

## 2. The Real Mission

The requested output is an event platform, but the organizational outcome is
larger.

KW Hab describes its work as person-directed. Its values include equity,
choice, inclusion, and collaboration. Its 2025 annual report states:

> The goal is not simply participation. It is connection.

The Belonging Collective grew from agencies facing limited resources and
increasing support needs. Its members explicitly wanted collaboration rather
than competition, including shared resources and stronger relationships among
frontline staff.

A strong concept should therefore optimize for at least one of the following:

- A person's ability to direct their own participation
- Confidence to cross the threshold into an unfamiliar event
- Natural relationships and repeated community connection
- Meaningful contribution, not passive service consumption
- Measurable collaboration between organizations
- Better evidence about unmet community needs

## 3. Live Calendar Findings

The public calendar is a read-only Teamup embed.

A fresh July 11 through September 30, 2026 snapshot contained:

- 219 displayed occurrences
- 52 distinct displayed titles
- 147 occurrences with no structured `who` value
- 11 occurrences with no notes or description
- Zero events using Teamup's native signup
- No Saturday or Sunday occurrences in that window

These counts are recurrence instances, not unique programs or attendance.

The live configuration mixes two different kinds of information into the same
set of coloured subcalendars:

- Organizations: KW Habilitation, DSO, Extend-A-Family, Independent Living
  Waterloo Region, Karis Disability Services, Our Studio, Sunbeam, and
  Waterloo Region Family Network
- Attributes: Free, Paid, Virtual, and Youth

The current calendar has no structured fields for:

- Accessibility accommodations
- Exact cost or support-person cost
- Registration requirement and deadline
- Sensory environment
- Quiet-space availability
- Step-free access or accessible washrooms
- Communication supports
- Support-person policy
- Arrival and check-in expectations
- Information owner or last verification date

At a 390-pixel mobile viewport, the embedded calendar still defaults to a
seven-column week. A representative event title was compressed into roughly 26
pixels of horizontal space.

The core problem is therefore not just visual design. It is a combination of
information architecture, incomplete structured data, uncertain access facts,
and a supply-directed model.

## 4. Winning Design Principles

### Start with one named user need

Do not design for a generic "accessible user." Low literacy, blindness, an
intellectual disability, autism, anxiety, and limited mobility can require very
different interactions.

Choose one primary scenario and demonstrate it honestly. Support other access
paths, but do not claim universal accessibility.

### Make accessibility part of the transaction

Accessibility should affect what information is collected, how a match is
made, and what the nonprofit must verify. It should not be a cosmetic toolbar
or an "accessible mode" added after the product is complete.

### Give nonprofits a concrete return

Potential incentives include:

- Evidence of real unmet demand
- Fewer repetitive access questions
- Fewer mismatched registrations or no-shows
- Better use of unused capacity
- Easier cross-agency referrals
- Shared rooms, staff, transport, or equipment
- Stronger outcome evidence for funders
- Trust from lived-experience review

### Make collaboration visible

Multiple logos in a database are not collaboration. A judge should be able to
point to a moment where two organizations share demand, capacity, trust, a
referral, or an event.

### Build for the pitch format

The concept must be understandable without a screen in 60 seconds. A strong
pitch normally contains:

1. One person
2. One specific barrier
3. One surprising reversal
4. One visible nonprofit benefit
5. One credible continuation path

## 5. Ranked Concept Portfolio

1. **Listen First:** People express demand before agencies decide supply. It has
   the highest originality and collaboration upside.
2. **First Five:** Discovery includes rehearsing the first five minutes. It has
   the strongest feasibility and accessibility proof.
3. **Needed Here:** People discover where they can contribute, not only attend.
   It offers the most memorable mission-level reframing.
4. **Belonging Relay:** Trust transfers from one agency to the next, making
   collaboration relational.
5. **Choice Kit:** A tangible choice produces one recommendation through a
   physical and screen-independent interaction.
6. **Open Seat:** People discover unused capacity available now. The idea has a
   clear nonprofit incentive and simple MVP.
7. **Collective Toolbox:** Agencies combine spare resources to create programs,
   making collaboration operational.
8. **Peer Postcards:** Self-advocates describe what an event feels like, using
   lived-experience trust instead of ratings.
9. **Plan B Network:** A cancellation produces one partner alternative,
   preserving participation across silos.
10. **Known Here:** Repeated places where relationships grow are prioritized,
    optimizing for connection rather than volume.

## 6. Deep Dive: Listen First

### Listen First in One Sentence

Most calendars ask people to adapt to what agencies already decided to offer;
Listen First lets people state what they want and gives the Collective evidence
to respond.

### Listen First User Scenario

Maya is an illustrative adult user who understands information best when there
is one decision at a time with picture, text, and spoken labels.

Maya chooses:

- I want to cook with other people
- Saturday afternoon
- A small or calm group
- Someone to explain what happens when I arrive

The product searches existing events first. If an appropriate event exists,
Maya receives one clear invitation.

If no event fits, the experience does not end with "zero results." Maya can add
her anonymous wish to an expiring demand cluster.

### Listen First Nonprofit Flow

The agency view might show:

> 12 people want quiet weekend cooking near central Kitchener.

An organization can:

- Respond with an existing event
- Create a new event
- Invite a partner to co-host
- Mark the demand as outside current capacity
- Ask for a missing resource such as space or a facilitator

The responding organization then posts the event with structured access facts
and its existing external registration link.

### Listen First Agency Incentive

- Demand is validated before staff spend time creating a program
- Similar requests can be aggregated for planning
- Partners can share delivery rather than duplicate it
- Demand and response counts can support grant and funder reporting
- Programs begin with expressed community interests

### Listen First Accessibility Proof

- One question per screen
- Picture, text, and spoken labels
- Full keyboard and screen-reader semantics
- No colour-only meaning
- A supporter can add logistics only after the person's own choices
- No diagnosis is collected
- No permanent profile is required for the prototype

### Privacy and expectation safeguards

- Aggregate requests only after a minimum threshold
- Show no names or individual diagnoses to agencies
- Expire old requests
- Make it explicit that a request is not a service promise
- Require opt-in before sharing contact details
- Allow the person to remove their request

### Listen First Prototype Slice

Build only:

1. A three-step accessible wish flow
2. A deterministic existing-event match
3. One deliberately unmet wish
4. An aggregated agency demand card
5. A nonprofit response and event-post form
6. The resulting accessible invitation

The prototype does not require a production recommendation model, accounts,
real demand collection, or automatic scheduling.

### Listen First 60-Second Pitch

> KW Hab is person-directed, but every calendar is agency-directed. Agencies
> publish, and people have to adapt. Listen First reverses that. Maya uses
> pictures or voice to say, "I want to cook on Saturday, in a small group, and
> I need help when I arrive." We search existing events first. If nothing fits,
> her request joins an anonymous demand signal. Agencies see that 12 people want
> the same thing, find a partner, and post one response. Maya gets a clear
> invitation with verified access facts. Most calendars help people fit
> programs. Ours helps programs fit people.

### Likely judge questions

**What if nobody responds?**

The interface never promises fulfillment. Requests expire, the user can still
see near matches, and agencies receive aggregate planning evidence.

**Is this collecting sensitive disability information?**

The prototype records situational needs such as "quiet group" or "plain steps,"
not a diagnosis. Agency reporting is aggregated.

**Can KW Hab maintain this?**

The initial version can remain a thin layer over Teamup. Matching can be
deterministic, and agency responses can remain human-approved.

## 7. Deep Dive: First Five

### First Five in One Sentence

Finding an event is not always the barrier; the barrier is not knowing what
happens during the first five minutes after arrival.

### First Five User Scenario

Lee is an illustrative person who needs predictability in unfamiliar spaces.
Lee chooses needs rather than disclosing a diagnosis:

- Calm arrival
- Plain-language check-in steps
- Step-free route
- A place to take a break

Lee discovers one matching event and opens its First Five preview.

### First Five preview

The preview contains:

- Photo and description of the correct entrance
- Step-by-step check-in sequence
- Host role and fallback contact
- Expected crowd, noise, lighting, and pace
- Quiet-space availability
- Washroom and mobility information
- Support-person policy and cost
- Whether arriving late or leaving early is acceptable

### Trust model

Never label an entire event "accessible." Every fact should be marked:

- `Confirmed`
- `Reported`
- `Not known`

Also show:

- Information owner
- Last-confirmed date
- Fallback contact

Wrong arrival information can cause more harm than missing information. Do not
call the data an "Access Promise" or imply certification.

### First Five Nonprofit Flow

While posting an event, the nonprofit answers four required arrival questions:

1. Where should a first-time attendee enter?
2. What happens at check-in?
3. What should someone expect from the space?
4. Who owns and can update this information?

Optional fields add sensory, mobility, communication, and support-person facts.
The nonprofit previews exactly what the community member will receive.

### First Five Agency Incentive

- Fewer repetitive phone and email questions
- Fewer avoidable arrival problems
- Fewer mismatched expectations
- Reusable venue information for recurring events
- Better AODA documentation and operational ownership

### First Five Prototype Slice

1. Choose two or three needs
2. Discover one real calendar event
3. Open its polished First Five preview
4. Demonstrate keyboard, read-aloud, and screen-reader semantics
5. Complete the nonprofit's four-field posting flow
6. Publish and show the updated public card

### First Five 60-Second Pitch

> Finding an event is not always the hardest part. For many people, the barrier
> is not knowing what will happen when they arrive. First Five lets someone
> choose what they need, not disclose a diagnosis: a calm entrance, a step-free
> route, or simple instructions. Every event then shows its first five minutes:
> where to enter, how check-in works, what the space feels like, and where to get
> help. Each fact is confirmed, reported, or not yet known, so we never fake
> accessibility. Nonprofits provide these facts through four simple questions
> while posting. We turn "Can I go?" into "I know how I will arrive."

## 8. Deep Dive: Needed Here

### Needed Here in One Sentence

Every calendar asks where someone wants to go; Needed Here asks where someone
wants to matter.

### Discovery mechanic

Instead of starting with event categories, a person chooses how they might like
to participate:

- Make something
- Choose something
- Share something
- Welcome someone
- Help with a small task
- Learn without taking a role

An event can offer optional roles such as:

- Choose the first game
- Pick one song
- Share a recipe
- Welcome a newcomer with staff support
- Water plants
- Demonstrate a hobby
- Help select the next event theme

### Nonprofit flow and incentive

While posting, the nonprofit adds at least one optional way for participants to
shape or contribute to the event. The role includes plain steps and any support
available.

The agency gains deeper participation, volunteer capacity where appropriate,
and better evidence of confidence, choice, contribution, and relationship
building.

### Critical safeguard

Participation must never be conditional on performing unpaid labour. "Just
attend" must remain an equal option. Roles should be low-risk, optional,
co-designed, dignified, and never replace paid staff responsibilities.

### Memorable pitch line

> Belonging is not merely being in the room. It is knowing the room is different
> because you were part of it.

## 9. Belonging Relay

### Belonging Relay Twist

Every trusted event can open one door into another organization.

At the end of an event, a person can opt into one clearly explained partner
invitation. The recommendation says why it was selected and can be delivered as
text, audio, or a simple card.

### Belonging Relay Nonprofit Incentive

- Reciprocal cross-agency referrals
- Attribution for successful introductions
- Broader reach without duplicating programming
- Stronger frontline relationships

### Belonging Relay Prototype Slice

Show Agency A posting an event, selecting one suitable partner invitation, the
person accepting it, and Agency B receiving a consented referral.

### Belonging Relay Risk

Avoid building case management. Do not share personal history, diagnoses, or
contact information without explicit consent. Full warm handoffs are an
operational pilot, not a hackathon MVP.

## 10. Choice Kit

### Choice Kit Twist

Do not make every person read a calendar. Let a person hold a choice.

A small physical kit uses cards differentiated by picture, shape, text, and
spoken QR output:

- Make
- Move
- Meet
- Learn
- Quiet
- Lively
- Today
- This week

Combining two or three cards returns one recommendation rather than another
screen full of filters.

### Choice Kit Nonprofit Incentive

The same shared vocabulary can be used in partner lobbies, community centres,
libraries, and support conversations. Anonymous card combinations also reveal
community preferences.

### Choice Kit Prototype Slice

Print a few polished cards. Each QR changes a simple digital matcher. Demonstrate
a keyboard or switch-accessible digital equivalent rather than claiming the
physical cards work for everyone.

## 11. Open Seat

### Open Seat Twist

Turn unused program capacity into a simple discovery channel:

> Three welcoming places are available today.

Agencies publish near-term capacity without rebuilding registration. The public
flow focuses on immediate, low-decision opportunities.

### Open Seat Nonprofit Incentive

- Fill already-funded spaces
- Reduce wasted facilitator or room capacity
- Reach newcomers without creating another campaign

### Open Seat Prototype Slice

The nonprofit marks three open places, adds a registration or contact link, and
the user's "Today" view updates immediately.

### Open Seat Risk

Capacity must have an owner and expiry. Never display availability that the
external registration system cannot honour.

## 12. Collective Toolbox

### Collective Toolbox Twist

The Collective does not only share events. It shares the ingredients required
to create them.

Agencies can offer or request:

- Accessible rooms
- Quiet spaces
- Facilitators
- Vehicles or transport capacity
- Equipment
- Volunteers
- Communication supports
- Community connections

A match can become a jointly posted event.

### Example

KW Hab has an accessible kitchen. Another organization has a facilitator.
Aggregated community demand shows interest in weekend cooking. Together, the
three inputs produce one co-hosted program.

### Collective Toolbox Risk

Real implementation requires scheduling authority, insurance, staffing,
ownership, and conflict handling. For the prototype, demonstrate one manually
approved match rather than claiming automatic dispatch.

## 13. Peer Postcards

### Peer Postcards Twist

Star ratings say whether somebody liked an event. Peer Postcards explain what
the event actually felt like.

Paid self-advocates can create short captioned and audio-described previews
covering:

- Crowd level
- Noise
- Pace
- Predictability
- Welcome and check-in
- What was confusing
- What helped

### Peer Postcards Nonprofit Incentive

- Trustworthy lived-experience feedback
- Clear improvement priorities
- Authentic promotion
- Better information for future attendees

### Safeguards

- Reviewers are paid
- One reviewer never certifies universal accessibility
- Content requires consent and moderation
- Reviews describe experience rather than judging disabled people or staff

## 14. Plan B Network

### Plan B Network Twist

A cancellation, closure, or full event should not send someone back to the
beginning.

The system offers one equivalent partner event based on activity, timing,
location, and selected access needs.

### Plan B Network Nonprofit Incentive

- Preserve participation after disruptions
- Share demand across agencies
- Reduce cancellation frustration

### Plan B Network Prototype Slice

Cancel one sample event and show one transparent alternative with the reason it
was selected. Registration remains external.

### Plan B Network Risk

The replacement must be current and genuinely comparable. Always disclose why
it was suggested and when its information was last checked.

## 15. Known Here

### Known Here Twist

One-off attendance is not the same as belonging. Prioritize recurring places
where relationships, recognition, and independence can grow.

Instead of recommending the largest number of events, Known Here looks for:

- Repeated gatherings
- Familiar venues
- Consistent hosts
- Opportunities to meet the same people
- A path from supported attendance toward independent participation

### Known Here Nonprofit Incentive

- Better evidence of connection and natural support
- Less emphasis on raw event volume
- Stronger alignment with Out and About's community-connector model

### Known Here Prototype Slice

Use one recurring live-calendar event. Show a first visit, a supported return,
and a future independent visit without introducing a gamified streak.

### Known Here Risk

Do not imply that formal support should be withdrawn merely because someone has
attended repeatedly. Independence remains person-directed.

## 16. Shared Event Data Model

The live calendar currently mixes publisher identity and attributes. A better
model should separate them.

### Core event facts

- Event title
- Host organization
- Partner organizations
- Start and end time
- Structured address or virtual location
- Recurrence
- Event owner
- External registration URL
- Registration required, drop-in, or invitation only
- Registration deadline
- Capacity

### Audience and cost

- Intended audience
- Age guidance
- Exact cost
- Support-person cost
- Funding or subsidy accepted
- Language

### Access facts

- Step-free entrance
- Accessible washroom
- Seating options
- Quiet or break space
- Noise level
- Lighting conditions
- Expected crowd size
- Event pace and predictability
- Communication supports
- Service-animal information
- Support-person policy

### Arrival facts

- Correct entrance
- Check-in sequence
- Host role or fallback contact
- Late-arrival policy
- Early-departure policy
- Transit stop or landmark
- Entrance photo with meaningful alternative text

### Trust and ownership

- Information state: confirmed, reported, or unknown
- Source organization
- Information owner
- Last-confirmed date
- Expiry or review date

### Participation and collaboration

- Optional participant roles
- Co-hosting needs
- Resources offered
- Resources requested
- Related demand cluster
- Suitable partner follow-up

## 17. What Not to Build

Avoid these as the core concept:

- Another filterable calendar
- A generic event chatbot
- Accessibility limited to font size, contrast, and read-aloud
- A single "accessible user" persona
- A replacement for every nonprofit's registration system
- A new manual CMS that makes KW Hab re-enter every partner event
- A podcast, postal-code SMS lookup, attendance streak, or flyer-upload tool
  presented as the entire twist; the organizers explicitly supplied those ideas,
  and KW Hab has already launched the podcast direction
- A broad AI platform whose output is not human-owned and verifiable
- A recommendation engine that silently infers disability or diagnosis
- Claims that an event is universally accessible

## 18. Decision Guide

Choose **Listen First** when:

- The team can tell a strong service-design story
- The team wants maximum originality
- The team can defend privacy, false expectations, and agency capacity
- The team can prototype both the community and agency sides cleanly

Choose **First Five** when:

- The team wants the strongest all-round rubric performance
- The team can make a visually polished arrival preview
- The team wants clear, demonstrable accessibility
- The team wants the simplest continuation story

Choose **Needed Here** when:

- The team wants the most memorable philosophical pitch
- The team understands the risk of unpaid-labour framing
- The team can co-design dignified, optional participation roles

## 21. Handoff Checklist

The final handoff should include:

- One clearly named primary user scenario
- Built versus future scope
- Architecture and data ownership
- Relationship to Teamup
- Relationship to external registration systems
- Nonprofit posting and approval workflow
- Data expiry and verification rules
- Privacy and consent boundaries
- Accessibility tests actually run
- Accessibility assumptions not yet validated
- Self-advocate testing plan
- Operational owner and maintenance estimate
- Failure states and honest unknowns
- The next three implementation steps

## Final Position

The room is likely to optimize the existing calendar. The strongest team will
redefine what the calendar is responsible for.

- **Listen First** makes programming responsive to people.
- **First Five** makes discovery continue through arrival.
- **Needed Here** makes participation become contribution.

That is the competitive territory: not more events on a screen, but more choice,
confidence, collaboration, and real connection.
